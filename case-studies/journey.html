Journey (YC W21) — What does it mean to tell a story well?

There is a moment

There is a moment, familiar to anyone who has sat across from a prospect, when the slide advances and something is lost. The conversation stops. The person in front of you waits. You are no longer talking — you are presenting. And presenting is a one-way act.

We believed this was wrong.

The Problem

Journey helped teams explain complex ideas at the moments that matter most — onboarding, proposals, introductions, the points where clarity determines outcome. Version one worked. It was clean, considered, and people used it. But it was still a deck: a fixed sequence, a predetermined path, a story that had already been decided before the reader arrived.

Stories are not linear. They respond, adjust, and breathe. This didn't.

The Shift

We didn't begin with features. We began with a question: what does it mean to tell a story well? Not slides or pages, but something that adapts to the person reading it — without a fixed path, able to go deeper or stay light depending on what's needed.

From that, three decisions followed. The format had to change. The story had to respond. And intelligence had to be fundamental, not layered on.

Removing Control

We removed the navigation. This wasn't simplification — it was a transfer of control away from the interface and toward the people using it. Without chapters or a forced sequence, the structure had to emerge from the story itself. In its place, we introduced a canvas.

A Canvas Without Shape

The canvas is unbounded and flexible, defined only by what the story requires. Text can sit beside video, tasks beside images, a booking flow next to a contract. There is no aspect ratio, because there is no single way a story should be told.

Everything Lives Inside

We rebuilt integrations so they were no longer external. Instead of embedding content, Journey absorbs it — a Loom recording plays as part of the story, a Calendly link becomes a booking surface, a DocuSign envelope can be completed in place. The story no longer sends you elsewhere.

This allows a single link to carry an entire interaction, from introduction to agreement to onboarding, without fragmentation.

Starting is the Hardest Part

An empty canvas creates both freedom and hesitation. We introduced multiple ways to begin: templates for those who want to move quickly, AI generation when the intent is clear but the form is not, and the blank canvas itself for those who prefer to think without constraints.

This wasn't about adding options. It was about recognising that people approach creation differently.

Intelligence, End to End

AI was introduced not as a feature, but as a layer across the experience. On the creation side, it helps shape structure and language, reducing effort without removing authorship. On the reading side, it turns the story into a conversation — a reader can ask questions and receive answers grounded entirely in the Journey itself, not drawn from the open internet, but specific to what the creator assembled.

Both sides express the same idea: intelligence in service of clarity.

Designed for How People Actually Read

On mobile, Journey becomes the screen. No visible edges, no slide boundaries, no interface chrome competing for attention. Navigation exists, but stays out of the way, surfacing only when needed. This isn't a responsive layout — it's a different way of thinking about reading.

Brand, Without Effort

Applying a brand becomes a single action. By pasting a domain, Journey understands the visual language and applies it automatically — colour, tone, and theme carried through the experience without manual setup. The goal was to remove decisions that don't add value and let the creator focus on the story itself.

Outcome

Journey became a single source of truth for customers. Teams used it to consolidate communication, reduce friction, and move faster through critical moments — sales cycles shortened, onboarding improved, and retention increased. It was recognised by Product Hunt and backed by Y Combinator. The product worked.

And Then We Stopped

Shortly after launch, the founders made a different decision. After assessing the market, they concluded that the opportunity was not durable enough to justify continued investment. They returned the capital, paid out the team, and sold the product.

The product succeeded. The business did not. Those are not the same thing.

Reflection

We underestimated the impact of the blank canvas. Although we introduced multiple entry points, we arrived at that solution later than we should have and without early enough validation. The AI assistant also shipped without a way for creators to preview its behaviour — they were placing trust in something they couldn't fully see, which created hesitation. We addressed this, but reactively.

I was also too focused on the product itself and not close enough to the commercial reality of the business. That's a perspective I've since corrected.

What Remains

Journey was built on a simple belief: the way we share ideas is broken, and it can be improved. We were able to demonstrate parts of that — a flexible canvas, a story that responds to its reader, intelligence that supports both creation and understanding. The business didn't last, but the ideas remain relevant.

What does it mean to tell a story well? We came close.
